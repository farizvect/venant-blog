At seventy, a corporate worker faces forced retirement. Human resources departments deem their cognitive speed too slow and their adaptability too rigid for modern execution. Yet, at seventy, a politician is just hitting their prime for the highest offices in the world. 

This paradox stems from a structural misalignment between execution and orchestration. 

In the private sector, value comes from execution. You solve problems, write code, and adapt to shifting markets. This requires mental bandwidth and stamina. A seventy-year-old cannot compete with a twenty-five-year-old in a sprint of raw data processing. 

A head of state does not execute. They orchestrate. They do not write policy; they approve it. They do not analyze data; they digest summaries provided by hundreds of advisors. Their job requires a different currency: power. 

Power accumulation operates on a decades-long curve. It requires time to build a political dynasty, secure billionaire donors, and consolidate factional loyalty. By the time a politician gathers enough leverage to run a country, their youth is gone. They trade their physical prime for institutional control. 

We judge ordinary workers by what they can do today. We judge global leaders by the influence they accumulated over the last half-century. 

The system is not broken. It is functioning exactly as designed. The elite prioritize leverage over competence, ensuring that the architects of the past continue to dictate the future.
