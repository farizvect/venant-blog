Our social algorithm is broken. If you choose honesty, people label you "self-righteous." If you choose cunning, they praise you as "smart." This isn't just a moral debate; it's a reflection of how our systems value short-term efficiency over long-term integrity.

The "self-righteous" label works as a defensive weapon. People feel threatened by honesty because it mirrors their own double standards. Attacking an honest person’s character is far easier than fixing one's own. Honesty becomes a nuisance for those seeking instant results.

On the other side, we praise cunning as a form of pragmatic intelligence. We romanticize the "hack" or the ability to exploit loopholes. In reality, being cunning is just technical debt. You gain an advantage now, but you're eroding the trust that keeps the system functional. Once that trust breaks, the cost to rebuild it far outweighs the profit of the original exploit.

Intelligence without integrity is just exploitation. Being clever means knowing how the system works. Being wise means knowing when to stop before you break it. If we continue to idolize the cunning, we shouldn't act surprised when the system eventually crashes for lack of an honest foundation.
